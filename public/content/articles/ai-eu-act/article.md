
How many Italian companies are already under the EU AI Act without knowing it?
According to ISTAT's latest report on enterprises and ICT, the share of Italian companies with at least 10 employees using AI technology has doubled in a single year — from 8.2% in 2024 to 16.4% in 2025. Istat That's roughly 200,000 businesses. And most of them have no idea what that means legally.
The EU AI Act entered into force in August 2024. Its obligations are not coming — they are already partially in effect. Here is what has happened, and what is coming next.

The timeline every Italian company needs to know
February 2025 — Already in effect AI Literacy became mandatory. Every organisation using AI systems must ensure that staff working with those systems understand their capabilities, limitations, and risks. This is not optional and does not depend on whether your system is high-risk. If you use AI and have not trained your team, you are already out of compliance today.
August 2026 — 5 months away Full enforcement for high-risk AI systems. This is the deadline that matters most for the majority of Italian companies currently adopting AI. Systems that evaluate people — for jobs, credit, access to services, or educational programmes — must meet a full set of technical and organisational requirements before this date.
August 2027 Obligations for limited-risk AI systems, including transparency requirements for chatbots and AI-generated content, become fully enforceable.
August 2030 Full application of the regulation across all AI systems, including those already in use before the regulation came into force.

The problem: fast adoption, slow awareness
The gap between large enterprises and SMEs is widening: in 2023 it was approximately 20 percentage points; by 2025 it had grown to 37 percentage points. Istat Large companies are adopting AI faster — and they have legal and compliance teams to manage the implications. SMEs are adopting it too, but without the infrastructure to understand what it means.
The most common applications are marketing and sales (33.1%), administrative process management (25.7%), and R&D (20.0%). Istat Several of these use cases — particularly any system that scores, ranks, or filters people — can qualify as high-risk under Annex III of the EU AI Act, regardless of whether the company intended to build a "regulated AI system."
Meanwhile, over 83% of Italian companies have not yet adopted any AI solution at all Giornaledellepmi — which means the wave of adoption is still ahead of us, and most of it will happen under a fully active regulatory framework that many companies are not yet aware of.

What this article is about
This is not a legal overview. It is a technical case study with real code: a RAG-based chatbot built for Founder Institute Philadelphia, and a detailed analysis of exactly what changes — legally and architecturally — when you add a single feature and deploy in Europe after August 2026.
The case study illustrates a pattern that applies to thousands of Italian SMEs right now: the line between a simple AI tool and a regulated high-risk system is often a single feature. And the cost of crossing that line without knowing it is not theoretical.
